What this actually does

This tool turns your text into a short sequence of tones between roughly 16.5 and 19.5 kHz — well above where most music and speech live, and quiet enough at the top of that range that most adults can barely hear it, especially through a small phone speaker. It is not silent and it is not real ultrasound (true ultrasound starts above 20 kHz, which ordinary phone speakers and microphones can't reproduce well). Think of it as a very high-pitched chirp, like the sound some chargers or old CRT TVs make — many people, especially younger people and anyone with sensitive hearing, will notice something.

Each character is mapped to a pair of tones (a simple FSK — frequency-shift keying — modem, the same family of technique old dial-up modems used, just shifted up out of the vocal range). The receiver listens with the microphone and uses a Goertzel filter — a lightweight way to measure how much energy is present at a few exact frequencies — to decode the tones back into text. A start marker, end marker, and checksum help the receiver know when a real message begins and whether it arrived intact.

Realistic expectations

This works best: phones held a few inches apart, speaker facing the microphone, in a quiet room, at full or near-full volume, with short messages (a sentence or two). It works worst: across a noisy room, with cheap speakers that cut off early above 17 kHz, or with very long messages where a single dropped tone can break the checksum.

If a transfer fails, shorten the message, move the phones closer, raise the volume, or switch to the more audible 16.5–18 kHz band in Advanced settings on the Send tab.

Hearing & courtesy

Some people — particularly children, teenagers, and people with sensitive or undamaged high-frequency hearing — can hear into this range. Pets (dogs, cats) can hear well beyond it and may react to the sound. Don't use this near anyone who hasn't agreed to it, and don't crank the volume in shared or quiet spaces.
